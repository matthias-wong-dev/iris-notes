Consider a manager faced with this decision: a team member has created software that solves a difficult organisational problem far better than anything available in the market. But relying on it would create a risk: dependence on an individual. Do you adopt it, or do you choose the inferior vendor product for maintainability?

One of the unspoken realities of organisations is that the need for safety governs far more than is visible.

I know this personally. When an incident occurs—say, a wrong number is reported in a high-profile context—the first question that appears is: *Was I responsible?* It takes effort to set that aside and turn instead to the questions that matter: *What is the impact? What now needs to be done?*

In itself, this is not unusual. What is interesting is that another question keeps returning: *Did I or my team follow the process?*

Why this question?

We all have a need for safety: emotional, social, physical, psychological. This need shapes behaviour everywhere. But when it is transposed into an organisation, it undergoes a shift in nature.

First, it is no longer named as safety. It appears instead as compliance, risk management, governance, roles and responsibilities, and clear process. These are standard management practices, and they can be legitimate. But at their centre is another function: when something goes wrong, responsibility—and exposure—is diffused. They do that by reducing reality into forms that can be managed—simplifying complexity into proxies that can be organised, distributed, and coordinated.

Second, in an individual, the opportunity to change course remains open. If individuals recognise they are heading in the wrong direction, they can revise judgement. But in an organisation, the mechanisms of safety have been *externalised* into structure. They sit outside any one person, and are therefore *un-revisable* by those within them. Unlike an individual, people in an organisation may experience a problem, yet remain unable to alter the system that produces it because the authority to change structures sits with another person.

The implication is that *the organisation mitigates pain by diffusing responsibility, and this diffusion becomes binding through structure.*

This is visible in major scandals. Boeing’s 737 MAX crashes killed 346 people. The company faced criminal charges, paid billions in fines and compensation, and entered into agreements with regulators. Yet no senior executive went to jail for the core safety failures. As of late 2025, the criminal case was [dismissed](https://www.npr.org/2025/11/07/nx-s1-5601593/boeing-737-max-crashes) via a non-prosecution agreement.

Or consider the Grenfell Tower fire. The Metropolitan Police [investigated 58 individuals and 19 companies for fraudulent behaviour](https://news.met.police.uk/news/investigation-into-grenfell-tower-tragedy-487374). As of now, none had been prosecuted. 

But this is not the most important part.

The Grenfell inquiry showed that the root cause was the failure of a fragmented system. Yet that fragmentation is precisely what makes prosecution difficult. Responsibility is so diffused that there is no single “designer of the flawed system” to hold accountable. The justice system can prosecute failures *within* the system, but real cause of the failure could not be prosecuted.

This is the need for safety working at maximum.

In these instances, the need for safety created [blind spots]({{< relref "blind-spots.md" >}}): responsibility is so diffused that signals of errors cannot be coherently interpreted.

Does that mean that need for safety is always harmful? No.

When a person needs to feel safe, one way is to avoid exposure to threats; the other way is to increase capacity to handle them.

The latter is what organisations in high-stakes situations do. The five principles of [high reliability organisations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High_reliability_organization)—nuclear power, emergency medicine, naval aircraft carriers—are:

1. Preoccupation with failure
2. Reluctance to simplify interpretations
3. Sensitivity to operations
4. Commitment to resilience
5. Deference to expertise

That is, an insistence on exposing the organisation to complex reality, rather than seeking comfort by containing it. This should not be surprising. We know learning requires exposure to pain. Many forms of safety tries to avoid it, while other ways of growing safety confronts it. And this is a dividing line between management decisions.

What causes blind spots then, is not the need for safety, but avoidance of pain.

On one hand, opting for a vendor diffuses responsibility for risk. On the other hand, it would be possible to say: *this capability matters enough that we will own the risks ourselves.* But the latter can only done with a will to increase capacity.

The task is not to abandon safety, but to recognise that staying adaptive requires exposure to pain, which our instinctive need for safety tries to avoid.

To ask, in each decision:

*"Are we optimising for safety, or optimising for outcome? Is it possible to do both?"*
